The First Law of Thermodynamics states that energy in a closed system is conserved and that the change in energy is equal to the heat added to the system plus the work done on the system.

 

Thermodynamic systems

A thermodynamic system is a specific region where heat, work, and mass are exchanged.

 

Thermodynamic processes: Adiabatic, isochoric, isothermal and isobaric

Thermodynamic processes refer to changes that occur within a thermodynamic system and are categorized as adiabatic (no heat exchange with surroundings), isochoric (constant volume), isothermal (constant temperature), or isobaric (constant pressure).

 

 

Heat capacities of an ideal gas at constant pressure and volume and relation between them

Heat capacities of an ideal gas refer to the amount of heat required to raise the temperature of a given mass of the gas by a certain amount (usually one degree). There are two types of heat capacities for an ideal gas: constant pressure (Cp) and constant volume (Cv).

 

The heat capacity at constant pressure (Cp) is the amount of heat required to raise the temperature of a given mass of gas at constant pressure, while the heat capacity at constant volume (Cv) is the amount of heat required to raise the temperature of a given mass of gas at constant volume.

 

The relationship between Cp and Cv for an ideal gas is given by Cp - Cv = R, where R is the gas constant. This relationship arises because an ideal gas does not occupy a specific volume and the internal energy of an ideal gas depends only on its temperature, not its pressure or volume.

 

In other words, the difference between Cp and Cv represents the change in internal energy that results from a change in pressure (or volume) at constant temperature.

 

Isothermal and Adiabatic processes for an ideal gas.

An isothermal process for an ideal gas occurs at a constant temperature, T. During this process, heat is added to the system, which results in an increase in internal energy of the gas. The amount of heat added is equal to the work done by the system, according to the First Law of Thermodynamics:

Q = ΔU + W

Where Q is the heat added, ΔU is the change in internal energy, and W is the work done by the system.

An adiabatic process for an ideal gas occurs without heat exchange with the surroundings. In this process, the temperature of the gas changes due to work done on or by the system. The change in internal energy of the gas can be calculated using the First Law of Thermodynamics:

ΔU = Q - W

However, since there is no heat exchange with the surroundings, Q = 0, and therefore:

ΔU = -W

 

In an adiabatic process, the change in internal energy of an ideal gas is equal to the work done by the system. The internal energy of an ideal gas depends only on its temperature and can be calculated using the ideal gas law:

U = nCvT

Where n is the number of moles of gas and Cv is the heat capacity at constant volume. By differentiating this equation with respect to T, we can find the change in internal energy:

dU = nCv dT

This equation can be combined with the equation for ΔU in an adiabatic process to find the relationship between the change in temperature and the work done by the system:

nCv dT = -dW

This equation can be integrated to find the change in temperature of an ideal gas in an adiabatic process. The result depends on the initial and final temperatures, the heat capacity at constant volume, and the work done by the system.
