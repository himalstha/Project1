Second law of thermodynamics

The Second Law of Thermodynamics states that the total entropy of a closed system will never decrease over time, and that the entropy of a system will tend towards a maximum value.

 

Thermodynamic systems and direction of thermodynamic processes

A thermodynamic system can have processes that occur in a specific direction, such as from hot to cold or from high to low pressure, and the Second Law of Thermodynamics explains why these processes occur in one direction and not in the reverse.

 

Heat engines

Heat engines are devices that convert heat into work.

Examples include internal combustion engines, such as the Otto cycle and Diesel cycle, and the Carnot cycle, which is considered the most efficient heat engine.

 

Internal combustion engines: Otto cycle, Diesel cycle; Carnot cycle

The Otto cycle and Diesel cycle are two types of internal combustion engines that convert heat into work. The Otto cycle is used in spark-ignition engines and operates on a four-stroke cycle, while the Diesel cycle is used in compression-ignition engines and operates on a two-stroke cycle.

The Carnot cycle is considered the most efficient heat engine, as it operates between two temperature reservoirs and converts heat into work. The Carnot cycle is idealized and does not correspond to any real heat engine, but it serves as a theoretical upper limit for the efficiency of real heat engines.

 

Refrigerator

A refrigerator is a device that removes heat from a low-temperature space and transfers it to a high-temperature space, thus cooling the low-temperature space.

 

Entropy and disorder (introduction only)

Entropy is a measure of the disorder or randomness of a system. The Second Law of Thermodynamics states that the total entropy of a closed system will never decrease over time, and that the entropy of a system will tend towards a maximum value.
